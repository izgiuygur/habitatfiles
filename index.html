The Endangered Species Act

One definition helped protect their homes.
It is set to disappear on September 14.

For decades, federal rules said significant habitat destruction could count as “harm” when it actually killed or injured protected wildlife. A rule finalized in July 2026 deletes that definition, narrowing a key protection for animals and the places they need to survive.

02The pivot

The fight turns on the meaning of one word.

The ESA makes it illegal to “take” endangered wildlife, and similar protections apply to many threatened animals. Congress defined take to include harm, but never defined harm itself.

Since 1975 — and in its current form since 1981 — Fish & Wildlife Service regulations have said that significant habitat modification can count as harm when it actually kills or injures wildlife by disrupting essential behavior such as breeding, feeding, or sheltering. NOAA adopted a similar definition in 1999.1

The new rule removes both definitions and adopts a much narrower reading. The agencies now say habitat modification or degradation does not qualify as “take,” and that take requires an affirmative act directed immediately and intentionally at a particular animal.

Until September 13, 2026

Significant habitat modification or degradation can count as “harm” when it actually kills or injures wildlife by significantly impairing essential behavioral patterns.

Beginning September 14, 2026

The regulatory definition is removed. The word “harm” remains in the Act, but the agencies say habitat modification or degradation does not qualify as “take.”

What remains

The Endangered Species Act itself is still in force. Species listings, critical-habitat designations, recovery programs, and existing permits are not erased by this rule.

Federal agencies must still ensure that actions they authorize, fund, or carry out do not jeopardize listed species or destroy or adversely modify designated critical habitat.

03What's already gone

For some listed species, almost all of their habitat is already gone.

Each outer square represents a species' historic range — or a particular habitat it depends on. The filled square shows the remaining share reported by the source. Because the figures come from different sources, years, and measures — total range, a particular ecosystem or habitat, or population abundance — they are not directly comparable, and the ordering here is not a ranking.3

The figures do not all measure the same thing. Some track total geographic range; others track a particular ecosystem or habitat type. Together, they show how little room some species have left.

The new gap may matter most on private land where no federal permit, funding, or other federal action is involved. Critical-habitat protections still apply to federal actions, but generally do not restrict purely private activity without that federal connection.

About the figures. The filled square is scaled by area, so its size matches the reported remaining share. The measures differ by species: total historic geographic range for the red wolf, Florida panther, and grizzly bear; a specific habitat or ecosystem type for the ocelot (Lower Rio Grande Valley thornscrub), salt marsh harvest mouse (San Francisco Bay tidal marsh), Attwater's prairie-chicken (Gulf coastal prairie), red-cockaded woodpecker (longleaf pine), northern spotted owl (old-growth forest), and Fender's blue butterfly (Willamette Valley native prairie); and population abundance for the elkhorn coral. These are snapshots drawn from different sources, baselines, and years — not a standardized ranking. Some of these species, such as the grizzly bear, have since expanded from their historic lows under ESA protection. Full sources appear below.

04What recovery takes

Recovery begins with somewhere to live.

None of these recoveries had a single cause. Habitat protection worked alongside captive breeding, reintroduction, pesticide bans, hunting restrictions, disease control, and years of monitoring.

Endangered Species Act protection was part of each recovery, giving species time, space, and coordinated support to rebuild.

The new rule does not erase those recoveries. It removes one way the ESA has restricted habitat destruction that kills or injures protected wildlife.

Each chart begins at a documented low, often before the Endangered Species Act was passed in 1973, and ends with the latest available count. The increases that followed reflect decades of habitat protection, captive breeding, reintroduction, pesticide restrictions, hunting controls, and other recovery work carried out under and alongside the ESA.4

05In fairness

The administration's case

The Interior and Commerce Departments argue that the old definition stretched the word “take” beyond what Congress wrote.

In their view, it turned indirect habitat effects into federal liability, left landowners unsure when a permit was required, and added unnecessary costs to farming, ranching, construction, fishing, energy projects, and other land uses.

The new rule, they say, restores a clearer boundary: acts directed at protected animals remain prohibited, while habitat is addressed through other parts of the ESA.

The economic costs were real in some communities. A 2021 study estimated that the northern spotted owl's 1990 listing and the protections that followed reduced timber employment in the Pacific Northwest and northern California by roughly 16,000 to 32,000 jobs, depending on the comparison method — while still finding losses far below the highest industry forecasts at the time.

Why critics say it fails

Critics argue that this legal distinction does not reflect how habitat loss kills wildlife.

A nesting tree can be felled, a spawning stream drained, or a migration corridor cut off without anyone touching the animal itself. Under the old rule, habitat damage counted as harm only when it actually killed or injured wildlife. It was never a blanket ban on land use.

Other habitat protections remain, but they do not cover the same ground. Critical-habitat review generally requires federal action, funding, or a federal permit.

The ESA's record is also hard to dismiss: fewer than one percent of listed species have gone extinct after receiving its protection.4

A coalition of conservation groups filed a federal lawsuit on July 14, challenging the new rule as contrary to the ESA and decades of legal precedent.

The story isn't finished

The rule is final.
The fight over it isn't.

The public comment period has closed. The rule is scheduled to take effect on September 14, unless a court blocks it. Conservation groups have filed suit. Congress can act, and states can keep or strengthen protections under their own laws.

1 The rule & the legal challenge

The rule. Rescission of the regulatory definition of “harm” — announced July 10, 2026, published in the Federal Register July 14, effective September 14, 2026. The notice confirms Section 7 consultation stays intact (agencies must still avoid jeopardy and adverse modification of critical habitat) and that Section 10 incidental-take permits will no longer address habitat modification. Announcement: U.S. Dept. of the Interior (official; supporters' framing). Analysis: Bloomberg Law · Harvard EELP tracker · supporters' view / jobs argument: Fox News.

The legal challenge. On July 14, 2026, Earthjustice and a coalition (Center for Biological Diversity, Sierra Club, WildEarth Guardians, Columbia Riverkeeper, Conservation Law Foundation, Conservation Northwest, Friends of the Wild Swan, Oregon Wild, Swan View Coalition) filed suit in federal district court in Seattle challenging the rescission — Earthjustice · NPR. The complaint invokes Babbitt v. Sweet Home (1995), in which the Supreme Court upheld the habitat-based reading of “harm.”

2 Species explorer

The species grid & taxonomic composition. Every dot is one of 1,643 distinct U.S. ESA-listed species, drawn from two authoritative datasets. 1,594 under the U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service come from the FWS ECOS species-listings-by-taxonomic-group reports (14 group files, deduplicated from listing-level rows to distinct species). 49 marine species come from the NOAA Fisheries species directory — the U.S.-range marine species NOAA manages, after removing 9 co-managed species (sea turtles, Atlantic salmon, Atlantic & Gulf sturgeon) already counted in the FWS data. Common names, scientific names, groups and current status are as recorded by each agency; the "Source" filter shows which. The NOAA data was verified row-by-row against the live directory (105 species / 174 listings — a 100% match).

Why the counts look different across sources. NOAA's directory reports "165 species," but that figure counts each listing separately — many species (Chinook salmon, steelhead, green turtle) are listed as several distinct population segments or evolutionarily significant units. Those 165 listings correspond to only 105 distinct species; the grid counts species, not listings. NOAA's own summary sentence (80 endangered / 85 threatened; 66 foreign) also differs slightly from its live table, which tallies to 81 / 84 and 69 foreign — the table (used here) is the more current of the two. The explorer excludes 3 "candidate" entries (not yet listed, so not protected) and 6 experimental populations (reintroduced runs of already-listed Chinook and steelhead, which would double-count).

Names, scientific names & status. Every name comes verbatim from the source agency's data; where a species had multiple listing entries (rangewide plus DPS/ESU or experimental populations), the strongest rangewide status is shown, grouped as Endangered, Threatened, or Threatened by similarity of appearance. Scientific names follow the official record, which sometimes reflects the taxonomy in effect at the time of listing (e.g. Physeter catodon for the sperm whale). Recently delisted-after-recovery species are absent because they are no longer on the list — the wood stork (delisted March 2026), bald eagle, Kirtland's warbler and snail darter — as is the monarch butterfly, which is only proposed, not yet listed (FWS).

3 Habitat-loss figures

Range- and habitat-loss figures (shrinking squares). Each shaded square is scaled by area to the share of a species' historic range — or a specific habitat it depends on — still remaining. The figures come from different sources, baselines, and measures (total geographic range, a particular habitat type, or population abundance), so they are snapshots, not a standardized ranking. Red wolf: >99% of historic range lost since ~1500 (Wolf & Ripple, Royal Society Open Science, 2017). Florida panther: occupies less than 5% of its historic range — >95% lost (FWS Florida Panther Recovery Plan). Ocelot (U.S.): over 95% of dense thornscrub in Texas's Lower Rio Grande Valley converted to other uses (FWS Ocelot 5-Year Review, 2025). Grizzly bear: reduced to ~2% of its former range in the 48 contiguous states by the 1930s — ~98% lost, though populations have since expanded from that low (FWS). Attwater's prairie-chicken: less than 1% of the historic ~6 million acres of Gulf coastal prairie remains — >99% lost (FWS). Red-cockaded woodpecker: the longleaf-pine ecosystem fell from 90M+ to about 5.2M acres — ~94% lost, and ~97% at its ~3M-acre low point (FWS, 2024). Northern spotted owl: roughly 85% of old-growth/old-forest habitat lost; FWS's 1990 listing cited a 60–88% decline since the early 1800s (FWS). Elkhorn coral: population declined ~97% since the early 1980s and is now under 3% of its former abundance — a population measure, not reef cover (NOAA Fisheries). Salt marsh harvest mouse: about 85% of San Francisco Bay's tidal wetlands — over 150,000 acres — were lost to development over the 20th century (FWS, 2023). Fender's blue butterfly: the native prairie of Oregon's Willamette Valley — its habitat — is down to less than 1% of its historic range, i.e. >99% lost (FWS, 2023). Two earlier examples were dropped for weak sourcing: the gray wolf's "90% of range" traces only to advocacy material, and the Florida scrub-jay's "90%" describes a population decline rather than habitat loss.

5 Limitations & editorial choices

An advocacy brief. No estimated or synthetic data remains in the explorer: every one of the 1,643 dots is a distinct ESA-listed species carrying its real taxonomic group, common name, scientific name, current status and managing agency, and the per-group counts are exact tallies from the FWS and NOAA datasets. Two caveats elsewhere in the piece: the "fewer than 1%" figure measures extinction prevention, not full recovery — relatively few listed species have recovered enough to be delisted; and the range-loss squares draw on different sources, baselines, and measures, so they are snapshots rather than a standardized ranking.
